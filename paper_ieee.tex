% ABSTRACT
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\begin{abstract}
Intelligent reflecting surface (IRS) assisted wireless communication is a promising paradigm for enhancing spectral efficiency and security against malicious jamming. This paper investigates the joint optimization of transmit power allocation and IRS reflecting beamforming in a wideband terahertz (THz) system operating at 100\GHz{} with 10\GHz{} bandwidth, where a smart multi-antenna jammer attacks legitimate user transmissions. We propose a hybrid reinforcement learning (RL) and alternating optimization (AO) framework: RL handles discrete power allocation decisions while AO optimizes continuous IRS phase shifts via closed-form updates. To cope with the dynamic and unknown jamming environment, we employ a fuzzy win-or-learn-fast policy hill-climbing (WoLF-PHC) algorithm that combines fuzzy state aggregation with adaptive mixed-strategy learning. The THz extension incorporates sub-connected hybrid beamforming at the base station, sub-array phase-dependent partial-connection (SPDP) at the IRS for wideband beam squint compensation, and OFDM signaling across 64 subcarriers. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed fuzzy WoLF-PHC approach achieves a system rate of 11.46~bits/s/Hz and 78.6\% SINR protection level, outperforming classical Q-learning (7.13~bits/s/Hz, 38.8\%), deep Q-network (7.68~bits/s/Hz, 41.9\%), and the AO baseline (8.01~bits/s/Hz, 44.8\%). Furthermore, sweep analyses over transmit power, IRS size, SINR threshold, and jammer power confirm that WoLF-PHC consistently dominates all baselines, with particularly strong robustness under increasing jammer power.
\end{abstract}

Anti-jamming, intelligent reflecting surface, terahertz communications, reinforcement learning, beamforming, beam squint, WoLF-PHC.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\IEEEPARstart{D}{ue} to the inherent broadcast nature of wireless channels, wireless transmissions are vulnerable to malicious jamming attacks where jammers intentionally emit interfering signals to degrade communication performance~\cite{yang2021irs,liang2019secure}. Defending against such attacks is a critical security challenge in both current fifth-generation (5G) and future sixth-generation (6G) wireless networks.

Traditional anti-jamming techniques include frequency hopping~\cite{hanawal2016joint}, power control~\cite{xu2018jamming}, cooperative relaying~\cite{li2015relay}, and beamforming-based approaches. However, these methods face limitations: frequency hopping requires extra spectrum, power control is energy-consuming, and active relays incur high hardware cost and complexity.

Intelligent reflecting surfaces (IRSs) have emerged as a cost-effective paradigm for enhancing both spectral efficiency and physical-layer security~\cite{wu2019intelligent,wu2021irs_survey}. An IRS comprises a large number of passive reflecting elements, each of which adjusts its reflection phase to constructively combine desired signals while mitigating interference~\cite{renzo2020smart}. The passive nature of IRS eliminates the need for active radio frequency (RF) chains, making it ideal for deployment in jamming scenarios where power resources are contested.

Yang~et~al.~\cite{yang2021irs} pioneered the application of IRS to anti-jamming communications by proposing a fuzzy WoLF-PHC learning approach that jointly optimizes power allocation and IRS phase shifts against a smart jammer. While groundbreaking, their work considered narrowband sub-6\GHz{} operation with a uniform linear array (ULA) at the base station (BS).

\subsection{Motivation and Contributions}

The migration to terahertz (THz) bands (0.1--10~THz) promises massive bandwidths but introduces unique challenges: severe path loss, molecular absorption, beam squint across wideband OFDM subcarriers, and the need for large antenna arrays with hybrid analog-digital beamforming~\cite{su2023wideband,yan2023beamforming}. These challenges are further exacerbated in adversarial environments where a smart jammer exploits the directional nature of THz links.

In this paper, we extend the IRS-assisted anti-jamming framework to wideband THz systems and make the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
\item We design a wideband THz IRS-assisted anti-jamming system operating at $f_c = 100\GHz$ with $B = 10\GHz$ bandwidth, featuring a 256-element IRS with sub-array phase-dependent partial-connection (SPDP) architecture for beam squint compensation, a 64-antenna BS with sub-connected hybrid beamforming, and OFDM signaling with 64 subcarriers.

\item We propose a hybrid RL + AO framework where reinforcement learning handles discrete power allocation (42 actions) and alternating optimization solves continuous IRS phase shifts via closed-form updates, achieving tractable optimization in the high-dimensional action space.

\item We employ a fuzzy WoLF-PHC algorithm with 3-feature state compression and 27-component fuzzy membership that provides smooth generalization across states and implements an adaptive mixed-strategy policy that is inherently unpredictable to the smart jammer.

\item We provide comprehensive simulation results including parameter sweeps over transmit power ($P_{\max}$), IRS size ($N_{\text{RIS}}$), SINR target ($\gamma_{\min}$), and jammer power ($P_J$), along with SINR cumulative distribution function (CDF) analysis and runtime comparisons, demonstrating consistent superiority of the proposed approach.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:system} presents the system model and problem formulation. Section~\ref{sec:thz} details the THz-specific channel model and wideband beamforming design. Section~\ref{sec:rl} describes the RL framework and the proposed fuzzy WoLF-PHC algorithm. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents simulation results, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{System Model and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:system}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{System Description}

We consider a downlink IRS-assisted communication system (Fig.~\ref{fig:system}) consisting of a BS equipped with $N$ antennas and $N_{\text{RF}}$ RF chains serving $K$ single-antenna legitimate users. An IRS with $M = N_1 \times N_2$ reflecting elements is deployed to enhance communication links. A smart multi-antenna jammer with $N_J$ antennas attempts to disrupt the legitimate transmissions.

The system parameters for the THz wideband configuration are:
\begin{itemize}
\item BS: $N = 64$ ULA antennas, $N_{\text{RF}} = 8$ RF chains
\item IRS: $M = 256$ elements ($16 \times 16$ UPA), $Q = 8 \times 8$ sub-arrays
\item Users: $K = 4$ single-antenna UEs
\item Jammer: $N_J = 2$ antennas (portable THz jammer)
\item OFDM: $M_{\text{sc}} = 64$ subcarriers, $B = 10\GHz$, $f_c = 100\GHz$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Signal Model}

Let $\mat{G}[m] \in \C^{M \times N}$ denote the BS-IRS channel at subcarrier $m$, $\vect{g}_{ru,k}[m] \in \C^{M}$ the IRS-user-$k$ channel, $\vect{g}_{bu,k}[m] \in \C^{N}$ the direct BS-user-$k$ channel, and $\vect{h}_{J,k} \in \C^{N_J}$ the jammer-user-$k$ channel. The IRS reflection matrix at subcarrier $m$ is $\bm{\Phi}[m] = \diag(e^{j\theta_1[m]}, \ldots, e^{j\theta_M[m]})$.

The received signal at user $k$ on subcarrier $m$ is
\begin{align}
y_k[m] &= \underbrace{\left(\vect{g}_{ru,k}^H[m]\bm{\Phi}[m]\mat{G}[m] + \vect{g}_{bu,k}^H[m]\right)\vect{w}_k[m]\sqrt{P_k}\,s_k}_{\text{desired signal}} \nonumber\\
&+ \underbrace{\sum_{i \neq k}\left(\vect{g}_{ru,k}^H[m]\bm{\Phi}[m]\mat{G}[m] + \vect{g}_{bu,k}^H[m]\right)\vect{w}_i[m]\sqrt{P_i}\,s_i}_{\text{inter-user interference}} \nonumber\\
&+ \underbrace{\sqrt{P_{J,k}}\,\vect{h}_{J,k}^H\vect{z}_k\,j_k}_{\text{jamming}} + n_k \label{eq:signal}
\end{align}
where $\vect{w}_k[m] \in \C^{N}$ is the beamforming vector, $P_k$ is the allocated power, $s_k$ is the unit-power data symbol, $P_{J,k}$ is the jamming power, $\vect{z}_k$ is the jammer precoder, $j_k$ is the jamming symbol, and $n_k \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2)$ is additive white Gaussian noise with $\sigma^2 = k_B T B / M_{\text{sc}} + F_{\text{NF}}$ where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant, $T = 290$~K, and $F_{\text{NF}} = 10\dB$ is the noise figure.

\subsection{SINR and System Rate}

The effective channel for user $k$ at subcarrier $m$ is
\begin{equation}
\vect{h}_{\text{eff},k}[m] = \mat{G}^H[m]\bm{\Phi}^H[m]\vect{g}_{ru,k}[m] + \vect{g}_{bu,k}[m].
\label{eq:heff}
\end{equation}

The received SINR at user $k$ on subcarrier $m$ is
\begin{equation}
\text{SINR}_k[m] = \frac{P_k |\vect{h}_{\text{eff},k}^H[m]\vect{w}_k[m]|^2}{\displaystyle\sum_{i \neq k}P_i|\vect{h}_{\text{eff},k}^H[m]\vect{w}_i[m]|^2 + P_{J,k}|\vect{h}_{J,k}^H\vect{z}_k|^2 + \sigma^2}.
\label{eq:sinr}
\end{equation}

The per-user rate averaged over subcarriers is
\begin{equation}
R_k = \frac{1}{M_{\text{sc}}}\sum_{m=1}^{M_{\text{sc}}} \log_2(1 + \text{SINR}_k[m])
\label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
and the system sum-rate is $R_{\text{sum}} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} R_k$.

The SINR protection level measures quality-of-service (QoS) satisfaction:
\begin{equation}
\eta_{\text{prot}} = \frac{1}{K \cdot M_{\text{sc}}}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\sum_{m=1}^{M_{\text{sc}}} \mathbf{1}\{\text{SINR}_k[m] \geq \gamma_{\min}\}
\label{eq:protection}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_{\min}$ is the minimum SINR threshold.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

The joint optimization of power allocation and IRS beamforming is formulated as
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
\max_{\{P_k\}, \{\bm{\Phi}[m]\}} \quad & R_{\text{sum}} \label{eq:obj}\\
\text{s.t.} \quad & \sum_{k=1}^{K} P_k \leq P_{\max} \label{eq:power}\\
& \text{SINR}_k[m] \geq \gamma_{\min},\ \forall k, m \label{eq:qos}\\
& |\Phi_{m,m}[n]| = 1,\ \forall m, n \label{eq:unit}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where~\eqref{eq:power} is the total power constraint,~\eqref{eq:qos} is the per-user QoS constraint, and~\eqref{eq:unit} is the unit-modulus constraint on IRS elements.

Problem~\eqref{eq:obj}--\eqref{eq:unit} is non-convex and NP-hard due to the coupled optimization variables and the unit-modulus constraint. Furthermore, the jammer's behavior is dynamic and unknown, making model-based optimization impractical. We therefore model this as a reinforcement learning problem with AO-assisted continuous optimization.

\section{THz Channel Model and Wideband Beamforming}
\label{sec:thz}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{THz Channel Model}

At THz frequencies, the channel exhibits frequency-selective behavior due to molecular absorption and beam squint. The BS-IRS channel at subcarrier $m$ follows a Saleh-Valenzuela model~\cite{su2023wideband}:
\begin{equation}
\mat{G}[m] = \sqrt{\frac{N M}{L_{\text{path}}}} \sum_{\ell=1}^{L_{\text{path}}} \alpha_\ell(f_m)\, \vect{a}_{\text{RIS}}(\phi_\ell, f_m) \vect{a}_{\text{BS}}^H(\psi_\ell, f_m)
\label{eq:thz_channel}
\end{equation}
where $L_{\text{path}}$ is the number of paths, $\alpha_\ell(f_m)$ is the complex path gain at subcarrier frequency $f_m = f_c + (m - M_{\text{sc}}/2)\Delta f$ with $\Delta f = B/M_{\text{sc}}$, and $\vect{a}_{\text{RIS}}$, $\vect{a}_{\text{BS}}$ are frequency-dependent array response vectors.

\subsection{Path Loss and Molecular Absorption}

The THz path loss at frequency $f$ and distance $d$ is
\begin{equation}
\text{PL}(f, d) = \left(\frac{4\pi f d}{c}\right)^2 e^{\kappa_{\text{abs}}(f) d}
\label{eq:pathloss}
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the speed of light and $\kappa_{\text{abs}}(f)$ is the frequency-dependent molecular absorption coefficient, which creates spectral windows and opacity regions in the THz band.

\subsection{Beam Squint in Wideband THz Systems}

A critical challenge in wideband THz systems is \emph{beam squint}: the spatial direction of a phased-array beam shifts with frequency. For a ULA with inter-element spacing $d_s = \lambda_c/2$ steered to angle $\theta_0$ at the center frequency, the normalized array gain at subcarrier $m$ is
\begin{equation}
\eta(f_m) = \frac{1}{N}\left|\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} e^{j2\pi n d_s \sin\theta_0 \left(\frac{f_m}{f_c} - 1\right)/\lambda_c}\right|^2.
\label{eq:beam_squint}
\end{equation}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:system}, for $B = 10\GHz$ at $f_c = 100\GHz$ (fractional bandwidth $\beta = B/f_c = 10\%$), edge subcarriers suffer severe gain loss. This motivates the SPDP-RIS architecture.

\subsection{SPDP-RIS Architecture}

To combat beam squint at the IRS, we adopt the sub-array phase-dependent partial-connection (SPDP) architecture~\cite{su2023wideband}. The $M$ RIS elements are partitioned into $Q = Q_1 \times Q_2$ sub-arrays, each containing $M_s = M/Q$ elements. Each sub-array applies a common true-time-delay (TTD) and per-element phase shifter, enabling frequency-dependent compensation:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{q,i}[m] = \theta_{q,i}^{\text{base}} + 2\pi f_m \tau_q
\label{eq:spdp}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{q,i}^{\text{base}}$ is the base phase of element $i$ in sub-array $q$, and $\tau_q$ is the TTD value for sub-array $q$.

\subsection{Hybrid Beamforming at the BS}

The BS employs a sub-connected hybrid architecture with $N_{\text{RF}} = 8$ RF chains, each driving $N/N_{\text{RF}} = 8$ antennas. The beamforming vector decomposes as
\begin{equation}
\vect{w}_k[m] = \mat{F}_{\text{RF}} \vect{f}_{BB,k}[m]
\label{eq:hybrid_bf}
\end{equation}
where $\mat{F}_{\text{RF}} \in \C^{N \times N_{\text{RF}}}$ is the analog precoder (block-diagonal, constant across subcarriers) and $\vect{f}_{BB,k}[m] \in \C^{N_{\text{RF}}}$ is the digital baseband precoder per subcarrier.

The digital precoder uses a max-SINR (MVDR) design. For each user $k$ at subcarrier $m$, the interference-plus-noise covariance matrix is
\begin{equation}
\mat{R}_k[m] = \sigma^2\mat{I} + \sum_{i \neq k}P_i\,\tilde{\vect{h}}_i[m]\tilde{\vect{h}}_i^H[m]
\label{eq:Rk}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\vect{h}}_k[m] = \mat{F}_{\text{RF}}^H\vect{h}_{\text{eff},k}[m]$ is the effective channel in the reduced-dimension baseband. The MVDR baseband precoder is
\begin{equation}
\vect{f}_{BB,k}[m] = \frac{\mat{R}_k^{-1}[m]\tilde{\vect{h}}_k[m]}{\|\mat{R}_k^{-1}[m]\tilde{\vect{h}}_k[m]\|}.
\label{eq:mvdr}
\end{equation}

\section{Reinforcement Learning Framework}
\label{sec:rl}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Hybrid RL + AO Decomposition}

The optimization problem is decomposed into:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{RL decision (discrete):} The RL agent selects a power allocation action from a finite set $\mathcal{A}$ of $|\mathcal{A}| = 42$ actions, comprising power fraction indices and distribution modes.
\item \textbf{AO optimization (continuous):} Given the power allocation, IRS phase shifts are optimized via closed-form alternating updates.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Power Allocation Actions}
Each action is a pair $(\text{fraction}, \text{mode})$:
\begin{itemize}
\item 7 total power fractions of $P_{\max}$: $\{0.2, 0.35, 0.5, 0.65, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0\}$
\item 6 distribution modes: equal, channel-proportional, inverse-channel, SINR-deficit, waterfilling, max-min fairness
\end{itemize}
yielding $7 \times 6 = 42$ discrete actions.

\subsubsection{IRS Phase Optimization via AO}
Given power $\{P_k\}$ from the RL agent, IRS phases are updated in closed form. For each element $i$:
\begin{equation}
c_i = \sum_{k=1}^{K}\sqrt{P_k}\,g_{ru,k}^*[i]\cdot(\mat{G}\vect{w}_k)[i], \quad \theta_i = -\angle(c_i)
\label{eq:ao_phase}
\end{equation}
which maximizes the sum of desired signal magnitudes. A secondary SINR-deficit-weighted AO iteration is also computed, and the strategy yielding higher sum-rate is selected (6 AO iterations).

\subsection{State Representation}

The high-dimensional observation is compressed into a 3-feature state vector $\vect{f} = [f_{\text{pj}}, f_{\text{ch}}, f_{\text{sinr}}] \in [0,1]^3$:

\textbf{Jammer pressure:}
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{pj}} = \text{norm}_{[15,40]}\!\left(0.6\overline{P_J}\,[\dBm] + 0.4\max_k P_{J,k}\,[\dBm]\right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Channel quality:}
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{ch}} = \text{norm}_{[-100,-40]}\!\left(0.75\overline{\|\vect{h}_k\|^2}\,[\dB] + 0.25(1 - \text{spread})\right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{SINR health:}
\begin{equation}
f_{\text{sinr}} = \text{norm}_{[-10,30]}\!\left(0.5\overline{\text{SINR}_k}\,[\dB] + 0.5\min_k\text{SINR}_k\,[\dB]\right)
\end{equation}

Each feature is quantized into $B_q = 8$ bins for tabular methods, yielding $8^3 = 512$ discrete states.

\subsection{Fuzzy State Aggregation}

For the proposed WoLF-PHC agent, each feature is mapped to three triangular fuzzy membership functions with centers $\{0, 0.5, 1.0\}$:
\begin{equation}
\mu_i(x) = \frac{\max(0, 1 - |x - c_i|/\Delta)}{\sum_j \max(0, 1 - |x - c_j|/\Delta)}
\label{eq:fuzzy}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta = 0.5$ is the spread parameter. The joint fuzzy membership vector has $3^3 = 27$ components:
\begin{equation}
\psi_\ell(\vect{f}) = \mu_i(f_{\text{pj}}) \cdot \mu_j(f_{\text{ch}}) \cdot \mu_k(f_{\text{sinr}}), \quad \ell = 9i + 3j + k.
\label{eq:fuzzy_joint}
\end{equation}

This provides smooth interpolation across nearby states, avoiding the discontinuities of crisp discretization.

\subsection{Proposed Fuzzy WoLF-PHC Algorithm}

The fuzzy WoLF-PHC algorithm maintains per-fuzzy-state Q-values $Q_\ell(s, a)$, a mixed policy $\pi_\ell(a)$, and an average policy $\bar{\pi}_\ell(a)$.

\subsubsection{Fused Q-Value}
The effective Q-value at state $s$ is computed as a fuzzy-weighted combination:
\begin{equation}
\text{FQ}(s, a) = \sum_{\ell=1}^{27}\psi_\ell(s) \cdot Q_\ell(s, a).
\label{eq:fused_q}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Q-Update}
For each active fuzzy state $\ell$ (where $\psi_\ell > 0$):
\begin{equation}
Q_\ell(s,a) \leftarrow Q_\ell(s,a) + \alpha\psi_\ell\left[r + \gamma\max_{a'}\text{FQ}(s',a') - Q_\ell(s,a)\right]
\label{eq:q_update}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.01$ is the learning rate and $\gamma = 0.9$ is the discount factor.

\subsubsection{WoLF-PHC Policy Update}
The win-or-learn-fast mechanism adapts the learning speed based on performance:
\begin{equation}
\xi = \begin{cases}
\xi_{\text{win}} = 0.01 & \text{if } \E_\pi[Q] > \E_{\bar{\pi}}[Q] \\
\xi_{\text{loss}} = 0.04 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:wolf}
\end{equation}

The policy is updated with step size $\delta = \xi \cdot \psi_\ell / L$:
\begin{align}
\pi_\ell(a^*) &\leftarrow \pi_\ell(a^*) + \delta \\
\pi_\ell(a) &\leftarrow \pi_\ell(a) - \frac{\delta}{|\mathcal{A}|-1}, \quad \forall a \neq a^*
\label{eq:policy_update}
\end{align}
where $a^* = \argmax_a \text{FQ}(s,a)$.

The average policy $\bar{\pi}_\ell$ is updated via exponential moving average, providing the reference for the win/loss comparison.

During evaluation, a mixed strategy of 85\% greedy (argmax of FQ) and 15\% policy sampling is used. This mixed component provides inherent \emph{unpredictability} against the smart jammer, a key advantage over purely greedy baselines.

The complete algorithm is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:wolf}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Fuzzy WoLF-PHC for IRS Anti-Jamming}
\label{alg:wolf}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Initialize:} $Q_\ell(s,a) = 0$, $\pi_\ell(a) = 1/|\mathcal{A}|$, $\bar{\pi}_\ell(a) = 1/|\mathcal{A}|$ for all $\ell, s, a$
\FOR{episode $= 1, 2, \ldots, E$}
\STATE Generate channel realizations, reset jammer
\STATE Observe initial state $s$, compute fuzzy vector $\bm{\psi}(s)$
\FOR{step $t = 1, \ldots, T$}
\STATE Compute $\text{FQ}(s, a)$ via~\eqref{eq:fused_q}
\STATE Select action $a$: with prob.\ $\varepsilon$ explore, else 85\% greedy + 15\% $\pi_\ell$
\STATE Execute power allocation $\{P_k\}$ from action $a$
\STATE Optimize IRS phases via AO~\eqref{eq:ao_phase} (6 iterations)
\STATE Compute beamformers via MVDR~\eqref{eq:mvdr}
\STATE Observe reward $r$, next state $s'$, compute $\bm{\psi}(s')$
\STATE Update $Q_\ell(s,a)$ via~\eqref{eq:q_update} for all active $\ell$
\STATE Update policy $\pi_\ell$ via WoLF-PHC~\eqref{eq:wolf}--\eqref{eq:policy_update}
\STATE Update average policy $\bar{\pi}_\ell$
\STATE $s \leftarrow s'$
\ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Reward Function}

The reward combines throughput maximization, power efficiency, and QoS compliance:
\begin{equation}
r = \sum_{k=1}^{K}\log_2(1 + \text{SINR}_k) - \lambda_1\frac{\sum_k P_k}{P_{\max}} - \lambda_2\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbf{1}\{\text{SINR}_k < \gamma_{\min}\}
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_1 = 0.5$ encourages power efficiency and $\lambda_2 = 3.0$ penalizes per-user QoS violations.

\subsection{Smart Jammer Model}

The smart jammer adapts its power and precoding based on observed user SINRs and the agent's action predictability:
\begin{equation}
P_{J,k}\,[\dBm] = P_{J,\min} + 0.25\,\text{clip}(\text{SINR}_k^{\text{prev}} - 5, 0, 20) + 6\eta + n_J
\label{eq:jammer_power}
\end{equation}
where $P_{J,\min} = 15\dBm$, $\eta \in [0,1]$ is the predictability score computed from the agent's last 20 actions, and $n_J \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 9)$~dB. The jammer precoder interpolates between isotropic and targeted jamming based on $\eta$:
\begin{equation}
\vect{z}_k = (1 - w_{\text{align}})\vect{z}_{\text{random}} + w_{\text{align}}\frac{\vect{h}_{J,k}}{\|\vect{h}_{J,k}\|}
\end{equation}
with $w_{\text{align}} = \text{clip}(2(\eta - 0.5), 0, 1)$. When the agent is predictable ($\eta > 0.5$), the jammer steers its beam toward users; otherwise, it jams isotropically.

\subsection{Baseline Methods}

We compare the proposed fuzzy WoLF-PHC against:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Classical Q-learning~\cite{watkins1992q}:} Tabular Q-learning with $\varepsilon$-greedy ($\alpha = 0.01$, $\gamma = 0.9$).
\item \textbf{Fast Q-learning~\cite{yang2021irs}:} Visit-count-boosted learning rate ($\alpha_{\text{fast}} = \alpha(1 + 3/(1+0.1N_{\text{visits}}))$) with accelerated $\varepsilon$-decay.
\item \textbf{DQN~\cite{mnih2015human}:} Deep Q-network with 2-hidden-layer network (128, 64 units), experience replay, and target network.
\item \textbf{AO Baseline~\cite{wu2019intelligent}:} Deterministic alternating optimization with fixed channel-proportional power allocation.
\item \textbf{No-IRS:} Direct BS-user links only (IRS phases zeroed), providing a lower bound.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}

The results consistently demonstrate three key advantages of the proposed approach:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Superior performance:} WoLF-PHC achieves 43--60\% higher rate and 75--100\% higher protection than all baselines, while the No-IRS baseline confirms a $2\times$ IRS gain.

\item \textbf{Robustness:} The mixed-strategy policy renders WoLF-PHC nearly immune to increasing jammer power---a crucial property for practical deployment against adaptive adversaries.

\item \textbf{Consistency:} Fuzzy state aggregation eliminates the initialization sensitivity plaguing tabular and deep RL baselines, enabling reliable deployment without extensive hyperparameter tuning.
\end{enumerate}

The $4\times$ training time overhead of WoLF-PHC (23.3~s vs.\ 5.9~s for Q-learning) is acceptable given the significant performance gains and is a one-time cost prior to deployment.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This paper has investigated IRS-assisted anti-jamming communications in wideband THz systems. We designed a comprehensive framework incorporating sub-connected hybrid beamforming, SPDP-RIS beam squint compensation, OFDM wideband signaling, and a hybrid RL + AO optimization approach. The proposed fuzzy WoLF-PHC algorithm jointly optimizes power allocation and IRS phase shifts against a smart adaptive jammer, achieving 11.46~bits/s/Hz system rate and 78.6\% SINR protection---improvements of 43\% and 75\% over the best baseline, respectively.

Comprehensive parameter sweeps over transmit power, IRS size, SINR threshold, and jammer power confirm the consistent superiority and robustness of the proposed approach. Most notably, WoLF-PHC maintains near-constant performance under increasing jammer power, a property attributable to its inherently unpredictable mixed-strategy policy.

Future work includes extending to multi-cell scenarios, incorporating imperfect channel state information, and investigating the near-field effects of large-aperture THz IRS arrays.
